Washington, D.C.’s dog park problem
D.C.’s dog parks primarily serve the city’s wealthy

Penelope Jennings

Washington, D.C. has 18 official dog parks, but they are concentrated in the wealthiest, and whitest, areas of the city. Wards 7 and 8 only have one park each, while Ward 3 has four. Ward 7’s Texas Avenue Dog Park is the only one east of the Anacostia.

The Texas Avenue Dog Park is also the newest in the city, as reported by The Washington Post in May 2024. The application for that park was originally submitted in November 2018, almost six years before it became a reality. 

According to the District of Columbia State Data Center’s 2018-2022 Key Demographic Indicators report based on U.S. Census data, 84.8% of Ward 7 residents identify as “Black or African American alone,” and 83.4% of Ward 8 residents identify as such. Both are by far the highest figures for any ward. In Ward 3 which has the most dog parks, 74% of residents identify as “white alone,” which is also clearly the greatest percentage of any ward. 

Wards 7 and 8 also have by far the two lowest median household incomes. Ward 7’s is $62,243 and Ward 8’s is $48,609. Meanwhile, Ward 3’s median household income is $141,393.

Ward 8’s Virginia Avenue Dog Park is the second biggest in the city at 12,600 square feet. Ward 3’s are all among the smallest, but the quantity makes up for their individual size. Wards 2 and 3 have the most total park square footage in the city, and Wards 7 and 8 still have the least. Ward 2 has three dog parks, including the city’s biggest in the 14,000 square foot Shaw Dog Park. 

Residents can apply to have dog parks in their area, but it’s a lengthy process. The D.C. Department of Parks and Recreation publicizes the necessary materials, and the applications currently under consideration. There are four potential parks listed as “under review,” with another lacking any designation.

If all parks were approved, Ward 1 would gain two more, and one more each for Wards 3, 6 and 8. The proposed park in Ward 8, Oxon Run Dog Park, had signatures collected in 2021 but lacks an official marking. It’s unclear when it would be developed, if at all. 

The application process is thorough. All of the applications have community organizations backing them. The application requires 50 signatures on the petitioners from area residents. The Columbia Heights Dog Park application has more than 500 signatures and is still not approved or officially recognized. It also has a letter of support from an Advisory Neighborhood Commissioner. The North Shaw Dog Park application has 272 signatures, and also does not yet exist. The parks have significant community support and still cannot reach fruition.

The Washington Post and DCist reported on the Oxon Run proposal in July 2021, almost five years ago, with no public progress since. 

“Ward 8 deserves the same amenities as other wards,” Parris Weaver-DuBose said in The Washington Post’s article. “Black people have dogs, too.”

Wards 7 and 8 have the least total space for dogs, and as a result, have to share with the greatest number of people. Ward 7’s park allots for 0.11 square feet of park per resident, while Ward 3’s allow for more than three times more at 0.36 square feet per resident. 

There is no public census data of dogs in the city, but Wards 7 and 8 still each have about 10,000 more human residents than Ward 3. 

As reported by The Washington Post and DCist, dog parks can spark a lot of community debate. Are they the best use of resources? Are they signs or causes of gentrification? Will they create excessive noise and barking? Any new space that is developed, or isn’t, will cause arguments. The creation of dog parks shows an investment in community, and the communities that are more heavily invested-in in this way happen to be among the wealthiest in the city.
