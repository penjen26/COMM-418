I think Upshot does fun stuff so I looked over there to see if they had done anything interesting lately. This subject also felt timely because my 38-year-old aunt just had her first child two days ago. While I think my family members are more likely than the average Americans to have children later (all of the adults have at least bachelor’s degrees, they’re liberal, the women generally stay in the workforce, etc) I’ve witnessed anecdotally that as generations move, people in my family are having kids later — my parents were 33 when I was born, my younger aunts and uncles have been older. I think the declining birth rate is a big conservative talking point so I appreciate the way this story poses two facts: yes the general birth rate is declining, but the average woman is still having around two kids, aka just above replacement level. The percentage of women ages 15-19 (girls) having kids has decreased a lot since 2007, according to the CDC, which is a really good thing. This data points to cool patterns and new abilities in society. It points to more freedom and choice for American women. The story points out that some of the decline can be attributed to financial woes, but basically all of the other reasons are great. The story goes back to teen pregnancy going down which is amazing. The data is all from the CDC, or UMD sociology professor Philip Cohen’s analysis of that data. I think the visualizations are pretty clear and show multiple sides of the declining birth rate story. I don’t see just an “average age of first-time mothers” in the story which I think would be useful – both the current figure and the change over time.
