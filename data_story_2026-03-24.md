The data is from the Energy Information Administration and shows the steep jump in the average price of a gallon of gas in the United States. The first graph shows the average percent change over four week spans from the past two years. The scale goes up to a 25% change so the differences look more drastic than they may on a 100% scale, but I think this fits for something like gas prices where an increase of just a few cents adds up when you have to fill a whole tank. The difference is also super clear when displayed this way. The jump is also contextualized in the paragraph right after — this is the “second-largest four-week increase in at least 30 years,” and is a bigger jump than those at the beginning of the war in Ukraine, or surges in 2009 and 1999. These changes in price are all associated with specific events – this one is tied to Iran, and the only other bigger change was due to Hurricane Katrina. Gas prices are a big issue that really affect regular people, and the story continues to contextualize it by showing the average spending increase for a month of gas for different kinds of vehicles. They also use state specific data from AAA, and every single state’s gas has increased by at least 54 cents since Feb. 18. I think the graph shown of individual prices a little lower down is somewhat confusing, but I guess the point is that there have been higher individual prices but this is the biggest jump.
